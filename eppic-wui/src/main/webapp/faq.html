EPPIC - Frequently Asked Questions

General

What does EPPIC stand for?

Evolutionary Protein-Protein Interface Classifier.

What can I use EPPIC for?

EPPIC mainly aims at predicting the likely quaternary structure of protein crystals. It bases the predictions on evolutionary scoring of the pairwise interfaces present in the crystal. In addition to that it provides information of general use for a number of structural biology applications:

  • Precomputed Multiple Sequence Alignments (MSAs) of closely related homologs (within 60% sequence identity) for every protein in the PDB or for user-uploaded structures
  • Colored-by-sequence-entropy surface representation in browser embedded NGL viewer. Very useful for looking at features present at protein surfaces: interfaces, binding sites etc.
  • Number and distribution of core residues in interfaces
  • Symmetry features of interfaces: the crystallographic operators generating each interface are provided and are depicted in red if they are conducive to infinite assemblies. Also, one can compare the left- and right-hand side of the Details table of each interface to see if it is isologous or heterologous

Is it open-source? Is it published open-access?

EPPIC is being developed as a fully open-source tool in the frame of the SNF-funded, use-inspired project "Molecular evolution for structural biology: analyzing and predicting protein-protein interactions". Since 2012 we have been following an open-access policy with regard to publications related to this project (see Publications page).

How should I cite EPPIC?

The main paper describing the EPPIC method is Bliven S, Lafita A, Parker A, Capitani G, Duarte JM, PLoS Computational Biology 2018. As well as citing the paper it is important that one cites the EPPIC software version (top right below the logo in results page) and even more important the UniProt database version (monthly releases) used for the evolutionary analysis. Our predictions depend on the sequence homologs found by searching the UniProt database so with every release of the database the predictions can change. UniProt is growing very fast these days so a few months of UniProt releases between two runs can make a difference.

How do I know if a prediction is reliable? Does EPPIC produce confidence values?

EPPIC produces estimation of confidences both for assemblies and for interfaces. There are 3 levels of prediction confidence: high confidence, depicted with a golden star ; medium confidence, depicted with a gray star ; low confidence, depicted with no star.

As well as the confidence values, there are a few useful indicators to understand if the prediction can be trusted or not:

  • The more sequence homologs the better: look at the number of homologs on the Sequence Information panel on top. If it is below 10 we will not even produce the evolutionary scores and the prediction will be purely geometrical (less reliable). If it is above 10 but not much larger than that one should be careful with the results.
  • The sequence homologs should span a wide range of identities: look at their distribution by clicking on the icon next to the number of homologs. A typical case is that of some microbial proteins where only very close sequence homologs exist (>90%) and then nothing else is found in sequence databases until <40% identity. The resulting alignment will contain less information, thus the prediction will be less reliable.
  • The stronger the consensus the better: if the three indicators are unanimous in their calls the prediction can be considered more reliable.
  • Non full-length proteins (domains or fragments) are less reliably predicted: you can find out whether your protein is full-length or not by clicking on the icon next to the chain name in the Sequence Information panel.
  • Check for any kind of warnings: low resolution or R-free warnings on top and various per-interface warnings in the last column of the Main Interface table. This latter category encompasses engineering artifacts (mutations, expression tags), possible artifactual mediating ions, engineered disulfide bridges etc. If any of these warnings appear one should always treat the results with more care.

Usage

What are the advanced input parameters for?

  • Identity soft cut-off: The soft sequence identity cut-off for BLAST hits to be used in the alignment. If there are not enough sequences at this cut-off then the threshold is relaxed until the hard cut-off is reached.
  • Identity hard cut-off: The hard sequence identity cut-off for BLAST hits to be used in the alignment. If there are not enough sequences at soft cut-off then the threshold is relaxed until this hard cut-off.
  • Max num of sequences: The maximum number of sequences to be taken from the BLAST hits.
  • Search Mode: The PDB sequence is first matched to its UniProt entry and that sequence is then used for blasting. In Global mode the full length UniProt is used, in local only the matching PDB part of the UniProt sequence is used.

Do I need to change the input parameters?

We don't recommend that the users change these parameters. We have used as default values the optimal ones found by benchmarking the method on validated datasets of biological interfaces and crystal contacts. Our precomputed results for PDB entries use always these defaults. A possible scenario where one would want to try other parameters is in the case of lack of sequence data within the default identity cut-offs. Predictions based on MSAs of too few sequences (<10 as a rule of thumb) are not reliable. When submitting my own PDB/mmCIF file, can I later access the results for the job? How about data privacy?

Every new job run on the server is assigned a unique job identifier, a long alphanumeric string that is only known by the user and that is extremely hard to guess, thus safeguarding data privacy. The jobs and input data are stored on our servers for 1 month and then deleted. It is recommended that you give an email address while submitting so that you receive the URL with the job identifier in your inbox. Otherwise, you will have to bookmark it or keep a record of it yourself. It is always possible to retrieve the job by using the URL http://www.eppic-web.org/ewui/#id/<my_job_id> . Whether the job is still running or already done, the URL will show its current status and automatically display the final results whenever it is finished. You can even share the URL with colleagues.

Can I directly link to the precomputed results for a PDB entry?

The PDB-wide precomputed results can be accessed directly by using the permanent URLs: http://www.eppic-web.org/ewui/#id/<PDB_code>

What does the UniProt version on the home page indicate?

The UniProt version (e.g. 2013_11) on the homepage denotes the UniProt version used to calculate the results of EPPIC. UniProt is used to blast for homologs of the input structure, which are then used to calculate entropy scores. These homologs can also slightly change from one version of UniProt to the next (monthly releases), so the results can vary over time. The UniProt version used for a calculation is also provided in the header of the Sequence Information panel in the results page.

What does the version below the EPPIC logo in results page indicate?

We always keep improving EPPIC with bug-fixes and new features. The number displayed below the logo indicates the version of EPPIC used to calculate the results. To find out what has changed in various versions, please refer to our Release Log page.

Where can I get the sequence entropy information?

A surface representation of each chain color coded by sequence entropy can be obtained by pressing "p" in the NGL 3D view window. The actual entropy values can be found in the Residue Detail tables (opened when clicking on the Details button in the Main Interface table).

What is the "Change 3D Viewer" option above the interface table for?

EPPIC lets one visualize the interfaces in 3D. By clicking on the thumbnails in the interface table one gets this interactive 3D view. The 3D Viewer drop-down menu allows you to change the type of viewer. The available options are: browser embedded NGL viewer (no need for local viewer) or a mmCIF file downloadable to a local molecular viewer, e.g. PyMOL.

Some assemblies have comma separated lists for size, symmetry and stoichiometry. What does that mean?

Those are disjoint assemblies where different components of the crystal form independent "disjoint" assemblies. E.g. in a crystal containing 2 protein entities, there is always a disjoint assembly formed by 2 independent monomers, one for each protein entity. The description for it would be:

  • size: 1,1
  • symmetry: C1,C1
  • stoichiometry: A,A

Does EPPIC work on membrane proteins?

Yes, we tested its performance on a validated dataset of membrane protein oligomerization interfaces and it exhibited satisfactory accuracy, though below that of soluble proteins. For further details see Duarte JM, Biyani N, Baskaran K, Capitani G, BMC Structural Biology 2013.

Does it work on antibody-antigen interactions?

Given the assumptions it rests on for evolutionary analysis, EPPIC will not reliably work on antibody-antigen interactions.

Does it work on engineered and/or chimeric proteins?

EPPIC will tolerate a small number of point mutations compared to the reference UniProt sequence and will list them in a warning message at the relevant interface(s). If the number of point mutations in a certain interface is too high, the evolutionary calls will be "nopred". Chimeric proteins will also have "nopred" evolutionary calls. Users, however, can edit out of the chimeric PDB file the component that is not of interest, thus obtaining coordinates that correspond to one sequence only. Those coordinates can then be uploaded to EPPIC and be analyzed. In any case it is not clear whether the evolutionary assumptions that EPPIC rests on, hold in the case of chimeric proteins. So predictions for these cases have to be taken with care.

Does it work on multichain NMR structures?

We expect EPPIC to work with reasonable accuracy on multichain NMR structures and we are currently benchmarking its performance in this respect.

Does it work on low resolution structures?

Predictions based on low resolution structures will be less reliable. Indeed if the X-ray resolution of a PDB entry is lower than 3.5 Å, a low resolution warning will appear in red next to the structure title on top. Whenever this happens one should treat all results with caution, since our predictions depend on accurate side chain positioning to calculate burial and assign interface core residues. A warning also appears in case the R-Free factor is higher than 35%. For structures with resolution worse than ~ 4.5 Å the results will not be meaningful at all.

Can I download the results data for my own analysis?

Yes. Since version 2.1.0 we provide XML data downloads. Find more details about it in the Downloads page.

Can I download mmCIF files of assemblies or interfaces?

Yes, you need URLs like http://www.eppic-web.org/ewui/ewui/fileDownload?type=interface&id=<PDB_code>&interfaceId=<Interface_id>&coordsFormat=cif

for interfaces and http://www.eppic-web.org/ewui/ewui/fileDownload?type=assembly&id=<PDB_code>&assemblyId=<Assembly_id>&coordsFormat=cif for assemblies. See the Downloads page for more details.

Can I download PDB files of assemblies or interfaces?

Unfortunately that is not possible anymore since version 3.0. The reason being that 1) large PDB entries can not be represented in PDB files, 2) EPPIC renames the chain identifiers of symmetry partners with original-chain-id_symmetry-operator-id, which results in long chain ids not representable in PDB files.

Method

How does EPPIC work?

EPPIC relies on sequence evolution and packing of the interface to detect the biological character of interfaces. The classifier uses a simple geometric measure, number of core residues, and two evolutionary indicators based on the sequence entropy of homolog sequences. Both aim at detecting differential selection pressure between interface core and rim or rest of surface. For further information please refer to Bliven S, Lafita A, Parker A, Capitani G, Duarte JM, PLoS Computational Biology 2018 and Duarte JM, Srebniak A, Schaerer MA, Capitani G, BMC Bioinformatics 2012.

What are core-surface and core-rim scores?

Core-surface and core-rim scores are the ones which rely on evolution to predict the biological nature of an interface. They are calculated as follows:

  • core-rim score: Ratio of evolutionary signal (sequence entropy) of core residues (at 70% burial) versus rim residues
  • core-surface score: A score (z-score like) of sequence entropy of core residues (at 70% burial) versus random samples of other surface residues

The scores are translated to calls using their cut-off values: if the calculated score is below its cut-off value the call is XTAL or BIO otherwise.

What does nopred in interface table refer to?

The nopred (no prediction) call can occur when there is not enough information available for EPPIC to reach a decision (usually not enough sequence data).

What do the core sizes in the interface table indicate?

Number of core residues (at 95% burial) in each interface, indicating how good the interface packing is.

What is the difference between core-geometry and core-evolutionary residues?

Core-geometry residues are core residues at 95% burial and core-evolutionary are the core residues at 70% burial. To calculate the geometry score, cores at 95% burial are used and to calculate evolutionary scores, cores at 70% burial are used.

How is the final call produced?

For interfaces the final call is based on computing a single probabilistic score based on the geometric and evolutionary scores. Probabilities above 0.5 are called BIO and below are called XTAL. For assemblies a probabilistic score is computed, representing the probability of a certain assembly to be the likely biological quaternary assembly. The assembly with highest probability is assigned the BIO call, all others are assigned as XTAL.
